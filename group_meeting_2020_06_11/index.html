Observables

Numerics: parameters $c$, solutions $q$, observables $O$: $$ c \overset{{\cal S}}{\to} q \overset{{\cal O}}{\to} O. $$

Can combine solution and observation operators, ${\cal L} = {\cal O} \circ {\cal S}$.

Example: airfoil, pressure $p$: $$ \text{Lift} = {\cal L}_1(c) = \frac{1}{{\cal K}(c)}\int_{\hat{S}(c)} p(c) (\hat{\bf n}(c) \cdot \psi_l(c)) \, \text{d} s. $$

The parameters or coefficients $c$ are assumed given in terms of a distribution. We want to compute them from the observations. The standard numerics approach is to solve for the flow field $q$ and computed all the things needed to compare to observations $O$ from the solution $q$ itself.

Observables are robust

The key is that observables are robust to training with very few samples (in this case). Producing the histograms of the pressure then computing the observables doesn't work without a lot more samples.
