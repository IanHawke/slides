Principles

Guzdial's principles for Programming Languages for Learners:

  1. Connect to what learners know (identity).
  2. Keep cognitive load low.
  3. Be honest (real).
  4. Be generative and productive.
  5. Test, don't trust.

Point 1 means we need to write a custom course - most are for CS, Physics or Engineering students. Point 2 means C, Fortran, Java are bad choices, and Mathematica problematic. Point 3 is about whether the coding is seen as broadly useful, or realistic. Maple falls here. Point 4 means the examples must connect to existing modules in interesting ways - it must be useful in ways pen-and-paper isn't. Point 5 links to MEQs and to trial run.

Material

Python material prepared, available online, multiple formats. Piloted on small group of UGs, PG demonstrators.

  • Covers numerical and symbolic topics.
  • Installed on University machines.
  • Free.
  • Used in eg Physics and Engineering.
  • "Proper" programming language.
  • Recognized by industry.

Alternatives considered were C, Java, Matlab, Mathematica, Maple, Sage, R. Most fail on cognitive load (C, Java) or "honesty" (Maple, Sage). R probably next best; Python probably has a broader user base.

Content

Mathematical examples, exercises show

  • Variables, control flow, functions, etc.
  • Symbolic methods.
  • Linear algebra, differential equations, statistics, plotting.
  • $\LaTeX$
  • Classes, testing, advanced topics.

Links to Number Theory, Differential Equations, Linear Algebra, Statistics modules.

More exercises are needed.

Feedback so far

Feedback says the material

  • is the right level for first year UGs;
  • will easily fill 12 hours of labs;
  • is well understood by the PG helpers;
  • should work on University UDE and students' own machines;
  • could use more worked exercises, to link to other modules.

The PG helpers checked over the summer of 2015, and looked at all the material. The UG students started looking at the start of semester 2 (roughly the start of February), and so have mostly only looked at the first quarter of the material. This has all been self-paced study with no help from staff.

Delivery

We can follow existing Maple labs route: self-paced with PG demonstrators.

  • No change in staff load.
  • Weak students will disengage.

Alternative model followed by Jacobs et al.: short live-coded intro, then hands-on exercises with demonstrators.

  • More "lecturing" preparation.
  • Expect much better feedback.

The Software Carpentry approach has a very good record in training novice level programming. However, it's usually preferred to have a 10:1 student/helper ratio, rather than current 25:1. Sticky notes and self-paced help but can't overcome this resource issue.
