All my research covers nonlinear numerical simulations of relativistic matter, particularly neutron star mergers.
My first current project is based on work with a PGR student who's just finished, Alex Wright, and one who's just started, Marcus Hatton, together with Bruno Giacomazzo from Italy. This approximates non-ideal physics, such as electrical resistivity, using an expansion about the ideal limit. This makes non-ideal simulations at realistic parameter values practical for the first time. However, there's significant numerical analysis needed in cases using gradient expansions, such as relativistic heat transport.
The second current project looks at the gap between the sub centimetre lengthscales where relativistic fluids are theoretically understood, and the much longer scales where numerical simulations currently take place. This leads to Large Eddy Simulation type formulations in relativity. This is not simple to implement in a coordinate invariant way. It brings in questions about stability, and can mask or confuse any true non-ideal effects. Much of this work is done by Thomas Celora, a PGR student of Nils Andersson, and also in collaboration with Greg Comer.
Finally, there's a question of how reliable our results are. Our simulations involve shocks interacting with fluid features on very small scales. This does not cleanly converge with grid resolution. Sid Mishra at ETH has characterised the convergence in a rigorous statistical fashion. However, for problems where the quantity of interest is a gradient, such as vorticity, this has significant problems. I'm working on this in relativity, together with another PGR, Grant Schomberg.
